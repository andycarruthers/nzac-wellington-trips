Finally, finally, FINALLYY!!!! After postponing five times since February due to weather we got away on a club rock trip on the last weekend of May.  Ten of us ventured north on a Friday night to Wharepapa South to sample the delights of Waikato ignimbrite. Getting up for an 8am start we found it to be somewhat...frosty? Like 2C, like I thought I took up this sport to get away from freezing alpine starts.  After 10 minutes on the Slug Wall (Froggat Edge) no one could move, all dexterity was lost to the fingers, and what looked like easy grade 15s became more like 19s. It's not that cold! Yeah right… Victoria, ShiChang, and Kevin discuss the first climbs of the morning.  Photos by Tommi Kokki. Still, it soon warmed up, and after an hour of sun and we were away. Lots of nice pockets, once you know where to look. Great exposure - after lunch the shirt even came off as we moved across into the Animal Biscuit Valley. My favourite climb = Bring Your Daughter to the Slaughter (18) a nice 25m overhanging arete with a tricky crux near the top (more like a 19).  Also liked Firestarter (17) and Moonbase Alpha (17).  Some good 15-25m routes here.  A full blue sky day with a light southerly made for perfect climbing which we ran out fully till it got dark (5.30pm). Day two was planned to be Castle Rock, but the rain had other ideas. So we bailed to Extreme Backpackers climbing wall in Turangi. Many of the routes had just been set so it took us a while to sort. One of the favourites had a huge 10m horizontal roof with big holds which had to be lead climbed - nice!! Kev Steve part way through his hour long jaunt ShiChang's view on a frosty morning on Bring Your Daughter to the Slaughter Sunset – still climbing Yep, while Kev’s crew were up at the crack of dawn braving the icicles on Slug Wall, Catherine, Marcus, Melody, and Ralf had a slightly more civilized start to climbing, heading off to Bayley’s Road for the day.  Started the day heading cross country to Monday Wall to check out the climbs there while waiting for the sun to warm us up.  A collection of climbs in the 15-20 grades with the odd 13 and 23 thrown in.  After taking note of potential climbs to try, or not (Nose Route, 23 “lock off on two large pockets, dyno, and then its all over one way or another…”) we headed back to the River Wall to climb in the sun, having the whole crag to ourselves. Attack of the Killer Oven Gloves, 20/20, Stuffed Toys - Rabid Ones in Lycra, and Ones With Attitude, alongside Sharp Aretes (grades 16-21) kept us all occupied providing us with some doable climbs and ones that were a bit more challenging.  The afternoon also saw Marcus take on Post Funeral Blues, a slimy 21 on the Roadside wall, and Ralph having a play on Swansong 20.  By all accounts the Blues could’ve been a nice climb on a summer’s day, but not recommended for last climb of the day on a chilly autumn day. Took another look at Gulp, the large chimney to the right of Post Funeral Blues, but recalling a less than pleasant experience on it on my previous visit and not having practiced my chimney climbing skills since, gave it a miss, ending the day. Ralf, Melody, and Marcus relishing the autumn weather at Bailey's Road. Photo by Marcus Manning. As the rain set in on day two, and Kevin’s car headed back to Welly via Turangi climbing wall, our crew headed back via Te Kuiti.  With Mangaokewa only a short distance from Te Kuiti and beckoning us to “Come Sample the Steepness!” a detour was mandatory.  As a first time visit for all of our crew we were surprised by what we found.  Having only really heard about the overhanging cliffs we were surprised to find a collection of easy grade climbs on the lower tier as well as the expected steep climbs sheltered from the rain.  With the limestone cliffs hidden in the trees, it had a bit of a Paynes feel about it but a few degrees colder and minus the hippies.  As we had already given up the idea of climbing for the day, this visit ended up just being a recce but one well worthwhile as we’d all made a mental note to come back another day. Catherine Ralf belays while the sun shines at Bailey's Road. Photo by Marcus Manning.
